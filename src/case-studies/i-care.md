## The Problem

Nurses are one of the most emotionally exposed professional groups — yet the structures for processing that exposure are fragmented, underused, or inaccessible during a shift.

Clinical supervision exists as a formal support mechanism, but in practice it often fails: sessions are poorly prepared, topics surface too late, and nurses arrive without the context they need to make the conversation useful.

## Research

I conducted **5 semi-structured interviews** with nurses across different settings — intensive care, psychiatry, general surgery, and an executive perspective. Key findings:

**Nurses follow unhelpful coping mechanisms by default.** Awareness of better alternatives (supervision, cognitive behavioural methods) exists, but time pressure and the structure of clinical shifts make them effectively inaccessible.

**Supervision is perceived as an added burden, not a resource.** Sessions feel unstructured, and nurses fear judgement within their team.

**Existing health apps don't fit the clinical reality.** They're designed for extended sessions, not 30-second moments between patients.

### Desk Research Insight

> Strong tools delivered without hospital context: mental health apps fail to support the fast, high-pressure reality of clinical work.

## How Might We

*We cannot solve the inadequacies of the healthcare system. So how might we change the way nurses deal with individual incidents?*

## Solution

The app has three angles:

1. **Staying aware** — a lightweight mood and health tracker
2. **Guided cognitive reframing** — an evidence-based CBT exercise that takes under 2 minutes
3. **Preparing for supervision** — topic logs that can be shared anonymously with colleagues before sessions

## Concept Iteration

I ran an **ideation workshop with nurses and a behavioural therapist**, then translated insights into user flows and three prototype iterations.

**Task completion rate increased from 45% (iteration 1) to 93% (iteration 3)** — a 48% overall improvement.

Major changes across iterations:
- Removed calendar, mental health offerings tracker, and multiple health app functions from the MVP scope
- Renamed exercises with verb-first labels
- Linearised the primary flow post-mood tracker
- Focused the core on: supervision topic gathering, incident documentation, and a short health monitor

## Key Design Decisions

**Writing topic logs** — Documenting incidents in writing helps nurses recall detail and process emotionally, without requiring real-time intervention.

**Quick reframing of thoughts** — Guided CBT in a structured 2-step flow: identify the thought, reframe it. Therapist-validated, minimal cognitive load.

**Publishing topic logs in advance** — Anonymous sharing before supervision sessions reduces fear of judgement and improves session preparation for the whole team.

**Streamlining sessions** — Capping supervision input to five topics, allowing time for urgent matters and ensuring all relevant voices can participate.

## Reflection

Working on a topic as personal as mental health — one I've encountered within the healthcare system myself — made this project both demanding and meaningful.

The biggest lesson: as someone who tends to solve everything at once, I went overboard with functionalities. This project reinforced the value of staying focused and continuously asking *why*. Rapid, low-fidelity prototype testing and a strong conceptual phase helped validate ideas earlier and concentrate on what truly matters.
